Overview

Happy Moves is a Virtual Reality (VR) rehabilitation platform designed to combine immersive 3D environments with evidence-based therapeutic principles. The platform facilitates neuro-motor re-education, musculoskeletal strengthening, range of motion (ROM) improvement, balance retraining and fine motor skill recovery through gamified, therapist-controlled modules. Motion tracking and immediate feedback promote adherence, enable objective progress tracking and encourage neuroplastic changes through repeated, purposeful practice.

1. Upper Limb Gross Motor Modules

These modules focus on gross motor control of the upper limb. Where noted, modules use an assistive volume — a virtual object (e.g., bat) that alters proprioceptive demands and engages both proximal and distal segments.

Mosquito Kill

Therapeutic Goal: Enhance upper limb motor coordination, endurance, and reach through repetitive target-directed movements.

Physiological Basis: Promotes activation of deltoid, biceps, triceps and forearm extensors while stimulating visual-motor integration and proprioceptive awareness.

Gameplay Description: Patients use a virtual bat to hit randomly appearing mosquitoes within a defined 3D space. The unpredictability of targets encourages wide movement arcs and dynamic range utilization. Therapist-adjustable parameters: target density, time duration, reachable workspace.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Post-stroke motor re-education
  • Shoulder mobility enhancement post–frozen shoulder
  • Functional training for upper limb coordination

Expected Outcomes: Improved arm control, increased ROM, enhanced reflex timing, and reduced spasticity through active movement repetition.

Fruit Cut

Therapeutic Goal: Improve gross upper limb coordination and motor control using bilateral and unilateral dynamic reaching actions.

Physiological Basis: Encourages neuromuscular reactivation of shoulder flexors and wrist stabilizers, and promotes visuomotor accuracy.

Gameplay Description: Patients perform slicing motions to cut fruits appearing in different positions, requiring quick, precise arm elevation and wrist movement. Therapist may configure unilateral/bilateral mode, target height and speed.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Neurological rehabilitation for hemiparesis
  • Orthopaedic rehab for rotator cuff recovery
  • Hand–eye coordination training

Expected Outcomes: Enhanced agility, coordination, and functional reach in upper limb use.

Balloon Pop

Therapeutic Goal: Promote upper limb strength and functional reach through visually guided target popping.

Physiological Basis: Stimulates motor planning, reaction time and sensorimotor integration through repetitive goal-oriented arm movements.

Gameplay Description: Patients pop floating balloons at random spatial coordinates, requiring vertical and lateral reach with controlled shoulder movement. Therapist controls balloon speed, size and reachable workspace.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Early to mid-stage stroke rehabilitation
  • Pediatric coordination training
  • Range-of-motion therapy post-fracture

Expected Outcomes: Improved shoulder stability, dynamic control, and reaction efficiency.

Blossom (No Assistive Volume)

Therapeutic Goal: Enhance controlled limb movement and spatial awareness.

Physiological Basis: Encourages activation of shoulder abductors and flexors, improving balance between agonist–antagonist muscle groups.

Gameplay Description: Patients reach out to touch or "blossom" flowers appearing in random positions within the virtual environment. Therapist sets target locations and tolerance zones to scale difficulty.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Upper limb ROM enhancement
  • Post-stroke spatial orientation training

Expected Outcomes: Improved flexibility, reduced rigidity, and better directional control.

Butterflies (No Assistive Volume)

Therapeutic Goal: Train smooth, controlled limb trajectories and midline crossing.

Physiological Basis: Encourages bilateral coordination and interhemispheric activation.

Gameplay Description: Patients interact with flying butterflies, requiring varied arm trajectories and sustained reach patterns. Parameters: flight path complexity, speed, and bilateral mode.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Pediatric and geriatric motor training
  • Post-surgical upper limb recovery

Expected Outcomes: Enhanced coordination and rhythm in motion.

Bubble Burst (No Assistive Volume)

Therapeutic Goal: Enhance movement speed, targeting accuracy, and reaction time.

Physiological Basis: Activates upper limb extensor mechanisms and refines visuospatial processing.

Gameplay Description: Patients pop bubbles appearing in unpredictable locations, demanding dynamic hand control. Therapist may adjust target size, time limits and reachable workspace.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Hand–eye coordination improvement
  • Cognitive-motor integration therapy

Expected Outcomes: Increased control precision and endurance.

2. Fine Motor Skill Modules

Picking

Therapeutic Goal: Refine finger dexterity and hand coordination for functional grasp patterns.

Physiological Basis: Activates intrinsic hand muscles and fine motor units responsible for pincer and tripod grips.

Gameplay Description: Patients pick up virtual objects with natural grasp-release movements. Difficulty can be graded by object size, weight simulation and required precision.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Stroke fine motor recovery
  • Hand trauma rehabilitation

Expected Outcomes: Improved finger control, grip strength and object manipulation skill.

Grabbing

Therapeutic Goal: Improve coordinated grasp strength and controlled release motions.

Physiological Basis: Targets flexor digitorum and thenar muscle group activation.

Gameplay Description: Patients grab and move objects, replicating functional daily tasks like lifting or holding. Tasks may require graded grip force and timed releases.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Orthopaedic wrist/hand rehab
  • Motor control re-education

Expected Outcomes: Enhanced voluntary grasp control and sustained grip endurance.

Eating

Therapeutic Goal: Restore hand-to-mouth coordination essential for self-feeding and ADLs (Activities of Daily Living).

Physiological Basis: Combines fine motor precision with proximal stability and visual-motor integration.

Gameplay Description: Patients pick virtual food with a spoon and bring it toward their mouth, simulating eating. Progression includes smaller items, timed sequences and dual-tasking (e.g., conversation prompts) for advanced training.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Advanced stage stroke rehabilitation
  • Parkinson’s or motor control impairment therapy

Expected Outcomes: Re-establishment of daily functional independence and coordination.

3. Neck Rehabilitation Modules

Neck Horizontal

Therapeutic Goal: Enhance cervical rotation and lateral mobility.

Physiological Basis: Activates sternocleidomastoid and upper trapezius muscles for improved control and posture.

Gameplay Description: Patients turn their heads side-to-side to touch virtual targets. Therapist limits range and sets cadence to control exposure and avoid symptom provocation.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Cervical spondylosis management
  • Postural retraining

Expected Outcomes: Improved neck ROM, proprioception and reduced stiffness.

Neck Vertical

Therapeutic Goal: Improve neck flexion-extension control and vertical mobility.

Physiological Basis: Activates deep neck flexors and extensors, enhancing cervical alignment.

Gameplay Description: Patients move their head up and down to hit visual targets. Parameters include target height, speed and repetition count.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Whiplash or neck muscle strain therapy
  • Postural correction exercises

Expected Outcomes: Improved cervical flexibility and control.

4. Trunk Rehabilitation Modules

Trunk Rotation

Therapeutic Goal: Improve core rotational strength and spinal flexibility.

Physiological Basis: Targets obliques, erector spinae and multifidus muscles to enhance dynamic trunk control and rotational endurance.

Gameplay Description: Patients perform controlled rotational movements to engage targets on either side. Therapist may set rotation amplitude and speed to match patient tolerance.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Postural imbalance correction
  • Core stability improvement

Expected Outcomes: Improved trunk control and reduced stiffness.

Side Crunches

Therapeutic Goal: Strengthen lateral trunk muscles and improve side-bending control.

Physiological Basis: Activates external and internal obliques and quadratus lumborum for lateral stability.

Gameplay Description: Patients perform gentle lateral crunches toward virtual markers. Progression includes increased range and repetition, with monitoring for compensatory movement.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Spinal alignment therapy
  • Core strengthening programs

Expected Outcomes: Better lateral stability and posture control.

5. Shoulder Rehabilitation Modules

Max Height

Therapeutic Goal: Restore overhead arm reach and improve shoulder joint mobility.

Physiological Basis: Stimulates deltoid, supraspinatus and scapular stabilizers to improve overhead function and reduce impingement risk when progressed appropriately.

Gameplay Description: Patients raise their arms to maximum comfortable height to reach virtual targets. Therapist can set safe ROM limits and graded progression to avoid pain provocation.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Frozen shoulder rehabilitation
  • Post-surgical mobility recovery

Expected Outcomes: Enhanced shoulder ROM and pain reduction.

Rotation

Therapeutic Goal: Promote internal/external shoulder rotation and joint flexibility.

Physiological Basis: Targets rotator cuff and scapulohumeral rhythm for stable, smooth motion.

Gameplay Description: Patients rotate their arms in a controlled wheel-turning motion to meet visual cues. Parameters include rotation amplitude, speed and repetition count.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Rotator cuff injury recovery
  • Shoulder stiffness management

Expected Outcomes: Improved functional rotation and pain-free movement.

6. Lower Limb Rehabilitation Modules

Dynamic Balancing

Therapeutic Goal: Develop dynamic balance, weight transfer ability and proprioception during movement.

Physiological Basis: Activates quadriceps, hamstrings, gastrocnemius and hip stabilizers to coordinate stepping and reaching tasks.

Gameplay Description: Patients perform controlled stepping and reaching motions to maintain balance and interact with moving targets. Therapist-configurable: step amplitude, target timing and assistive support.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Early gait training
  • Post-stroke balance retraining

Expected Outcomes: Enhanced stability, confidence and gait symmetry.

Static Balancing

Therapeutic Goal: Improve static balance and core–leg coordination for standing stability.

Physiological Basis: Promotes neuromuscular coordination and weight distribution control across lower limb and trunk muscles.

Gameplay Description: Patients practice standing balance with therapist guidance, shifting weight gently between legs. Progression includes reduced base-of-support and timed holds.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Pre-gait rehabilitation
  • Lower limb strengthening

Expected Outcomes: Increased postural stability and confidence during standing tasks.

Side Marches

Therapeutic Goal: Enhance lateral mobility and hip joint flexibility through side-stepping motions.

Physiological Basis: Strengthens hip abductors, adductors and gluteus medius — muscles vital for lateral stability and safe gait transitions.

Gameplay Description: Patients perform sideward steps to interact with objects, improving controlled movement. Therapist may configure cadence, step width and progression complexity.

Clinical Use Cases:

  • Gait correction therapy
  • Parkinson’s or stroke movement training

Expected Outcomes: Improved lateral stability and dynamic gait balance.

How data is captured

Each gameplay instance is stored as an individual session record tied to the patient ID and timestamp. Records include: motion tracking frames, joint coordinates, timestamps, game events (targets hit/missed), and therapist metadata (difficulty, assistive volume used).

Session storage

  • Session metadata (patient, date/time, module, settings)
  • Frame-by-frame kinematics (XYZ coordinates, joint angles)
  • Event logs (targets, scores, timestamps)
  • Derived metrics (range-of-motion, efficiency, reaction time)

Performance Visualization

For each session, the system displays the exact points where targets appeared and how efficiently the patient interacted with them. Each point is represented by a colored marker, where the colour intensity indicates the efficiency level - based on how quickly and accurately the target was reached.

  • Green points : targets reached efficiently with accurate movement
  • Yellow points : moderate efficiency, slight delay or deviation
  • Red points : low efficiency, missed or delayed reach

The visual provides a clear, point-by-point summary of performance - helping therapists instantly identify strong and weak areas in the patient’s reach or coordination pattern.

Clinical utility

Therapists can use session and trend analytics to:

  • Monitor gradual improvement in movement and accuracy
  • Identify when progress slows or needs adjustment
  • Compare performance between both hands
  • Use data to support therapy decisions

Conclusion

Happy Moves integrates modern VR technology with therapeutic science to support patient-centred rehabilitation. The platform offers therapist-controlled sessions, objective progress tracking and graded difficulty-enabling clinicians to prescribe targeted, measurable therapy that promotes neuroplasticity and functional recovery. By making rehabilitation immersive and engaging, Happy Moves improves adherence and supports faster, more sustainable outcomes.
